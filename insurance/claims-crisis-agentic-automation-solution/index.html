The Claims Crisis No One Can Ignore and How Agentic Automation Solves It

"Claims are where insurers either earn trust, or quietly lose it."

That idea has held true for decades. What has changed is the nature of the claims themselves, and increasingly, the way forward is being shaped by agentic AI.

Modern claims are no longer predictable, document-driven transactions. They are layered, context-heavy decisions. They involve multiple data sources, conditional logic, and increasing regulatory scrutiny. Yet most claims processing systems in insurance environments still operate like linear workflows.

That mismatch is not immediately visible. But it shows up in cycle times, in leakage, and in the growing gap between customer expectations and actual delivery.

The system is not broken. It is simply operating beyond what it was designed to handle.

The Illusion of Progress Through Digitization

Everything is digital. So why does it still feel slow?

Because digitization has largely improved how claims enter the system, not how they are resolved within it.

Front-end experiences have evolved. FNOL is faster. Document submission is seamless. Intake is no longer the bottleneck.

But once a claim moves inward, it encounters fragmentation. Policy validation, customer data, fraud checks, and payment systems continue to operate in silos. Each step requires coordination. Each interaction introduces a delay — not always visible in isolation, but significant when combined.

Most insurance claims management software environments have been expanded over time rather than reimagined. Layers have been added, but the underlying fragmentation remains intact. So while input speed has increased, resolution speed has not kept pace.

Where the Real Bottleneck Exists: Decision-Making

Why does decision-making still depend so heavily on people? Because traditional claims processing software is designed to move data rather than interpret it.

Every claim carries some level of ambiguity. Coverage needs to be interpreted. Documents may not align perfectly. Patterns need to be recognized across historical data. These are not binary checks — they are contextual decisions.

Rule-based systems attempt to manage this complexity, but rules only go so far. They work in predictable scenarios. They struggle with nuance. As complexity increases, systems escalate — not because every case is truly complex, but because the system cannot resolve uncertainty on its own.

This is where human dependency persists. And this is where scale becomes difficult.

Understanding the Real Crisis: Decision Latency

The industry often talks about processing delays. A more precise way to frame the issue is decision latency.

Data moves quickly across systems. But decisions take time because they require assembling context from multiple sources. Each pause, each validation, each escalation, and each manual review adds to the total time required to resolve a claim.

These delays are not just operational concerns. They introduce financial leakage, increase operational costs, and create inconsistent customer experiences. The system is efficient in execution. It is slow in conclusion.

Why Traditional Automation Hasn't Solved It

Automation has delivered value, but mostly at the task level. Data extraction has improved. Workflows have been streamlined. Repetitive actions have been reduced. These changes matter.

But they do not fundamentally alter how decisions are made. Most automation efforts have focused on doing existing tasks faster. They have not addressed the need for systems capable of evaluating, interpreting, and deciding with greater autonomy.

That gap is why, despite investment, many organizations still experience the same structural challenges.

Agentic Automation: Moving from Execution to Intelligence

This is where agentic automation changes the equation. According to Deloitte, nearly 70% of health plans are already prioritizing agentic AI across utilization management, prior authorization, and claims management. This is no longer a future bet — it is a direct response to the growing limits of execution-focused automation.

Instead of treating claims as a sequence of tasks, agentic automation treats them as situations that need to be evaluated in context. An agentic system does not simply follow predefined workflows. It interprets incoming data, links it to relevant context, and dynamically determines the next best action.

If a claim is straightforward, it moves toward resolution without delay. If inconsistencies appear, the system investigates further. If risk signals emerge, escalation happens with context already in place. The process becomes adaptive rather than fixed.

This is a meaningful shift for modern insurance claims software — from execution-focused systems to decision-oriented ones.

What Changes in Practice

"AI agents offer a new, very flexible way of patching any problems, either by automating steps, or by handling exceptions more flexibly." — Timo Elliott, BP Marketing and Global Innovation Evangelist for SAP

The most noticeable change is not speed alone, but flow. Routine claims no longer get caught in unnecessary loops — they move through the system with minimal friction. At the same time, complex claims receive more focused attention, with relevant data already assembled.

Unstructured data, long a constraint, becomes more usable. Instead of simply extracting fields from documents, the system begins to interpret meaning — connecting narratives, identifying inconsistencies, and aligning them with policy terms.

Human involvement becomes more precise. Effort shifts from handling volume to addressing exceptions that genuinely require judgment. The system starts to behave less like a pipeline and more like an adaptive network.

Rethinking the Role of Existing Systems

A common concern at the leadership level is whether this requires replacing the current infrastructure. In most cases, it does not.

Agentic automation can operate as an orchestration layer across existing insurance claims processing systems. It connects systems, aligns data flows, and introduces intelligence without requiring a complete rebuild. This allows organizations to modernize incrementally while preserving prior investments. The transformation is less about replacement and more about enablement.

The Strategic Implication

Claims is no longer just an operational function. It is where customer expectations are tested, where fraud risks are identified, and where financial outcomes are determined. It is also becoming a critical source of insight for underwriting and product design.

Improving claims, therefore, is not just about reducing cost. It is about strengthening the entire insurance value chain. Organizations that address decision latency gain more than efficiency — they gain consistency, agility, and control.

From Claims Processing to Claims Intelligence

There is a broader shift underway, one that goes beyond efficiency or cost optimization. Claims functions are beginning to evolve into intelligence engines.

Every interaction within a modern insurance claims processing system generates signals — patterns around fraud, indicators of underwriting gaps, insights into customer behavior and product performance. Historically, much of this data remained underutilized, locked within fragmented ecosystems.

Agentic automation changes how that data is used. Instead of processing claims and moving on, the AI-powered claims management system continuously learns. It connects outcomes with inputs. It identifies recurring anomalies. It surfaces trends that would otherwise remain invisible.

Underwriting becomes more precise because it is informed by real-time loss patterns. Product design improves because claims data highlights coverage gaps and customer pain points. Risk models become more dynamic, adjusting not annually, but continuously.

In that sense, modern insurance claims software is no longer just about resolution. It becomes a feedback loop — one that strengthens decision-making across the enterprise. And that is where the real advantage begins to emerge.

A Closing Thought

The claims crisis is not defined solely by volume or complexity. It is defined by the growing gap between how claims need to be handled and how systems are currently designed to handle them.

Agentic automation does not simply make existing processes faster. It enables systems to think, adapt, and resolve with greater confidence. And in doing so, it addresses the one issue that has quietly limited claims transformation for years: not how quickly a claim moves, but how effectively it is decided.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is decision latency in insurance claims?

Decision latency is the time lost assembling context from multiple data sources before a claims decision can be made. Unlike simple processing delays, decision latency stems from fragmented systems that cannot interpret or resolve ambiguity autonomously.

How does agentic automation differ from traditional claims automation?

Traditional automation speeds up individual tasks within fixed workflows. Agentic automation interprets incoming data, links it to relevant context, and dynamically determines the next best action — moving from execution-focused to decision-oriented processing.

Does agentic automation require replacing existing claims systems?

No. Agentic automation typically operates as an orchestration layer across existing insurance claims processing systems, connecting data flows and introducing intelligence without requiring a full infrastructure rebuild.

What is claims intelligence and why does it matter?

Claims intelligence refers to the continuous use of claims data — fraud patterns, underwriting gaps, customer behavior signals — to improve decision-making across the enterprise. It turns the claims function from a cost center into a strategic feedback loop.
